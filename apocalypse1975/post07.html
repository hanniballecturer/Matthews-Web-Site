Out of the Frying Pan

“If you want a picture of the future, imagine a boot stamping on a human face - forever.”

George Orwell’s 1984 is a good place to start for understanding how it feels to be in a cult.

Forbidden acts go on in secret, members are constantly surveilled, history is eternally re-written and relationships, especially sexual ones are strictly controlled. Orwell creates a strong sense of being inside looking out. The disdain for the non party members, the proles, reflects the attitudes cult members may have to outsiders. Lastly there is the strong leader figure, Big Brother, who is constantly imprinted on each member’s heart and mind.

Orwell gets one thing wrong though when he comes to the process of thought reform or brain washing. To undergo thought reform there is no need to be tied to a chair or exposed to your worst nightmares in room 101.

The simplest mechanism for thought reform is an age old technique called lying.

In this modern age lying is now seen as a valid political strategy.

Good liars make for good cult leaders, they can make people believe any crap they like.

At the very least they make you doubt yourself and the worth of your own values.

Cult leaders are in my view likely to be pathological liars. They may be charming, charismatic, funny and speak with genuine conviction. They speak with great confidence about what they believe, and can be very persuasive. You may mistake their charm, conviction and confidence as truth. More often than not they are gas lighting you. When they adjust the truth they may imagine the truth is being adjusted to protect you. They imagine the truth is being released incrementally such that you understand each aspect "correctly".

If you are unable to make an informed choice about what you are getting into then you are very likely to be mislead. Lying, deception, bending the truth, keeping silent about wrong doing. They all serve to provide false information as you trustingly cross the threshold.

A second effective tool for thought reform is to use the people around you to force conformity.

The people you trust. There is something primal about the human need to follow the herd. I once went to a meeting of the Jesus Army in Leicester. A friend of mine was doing research into forms of church music for their undergraduate dissertation. We entered the tent and they said we should sit at the front. “Why” I asked? “Because if you sit further back you are likely be swept up by the atmosphere in the room!” She was right. Even sitting where we were had a profound impact on me. I couldn’t sleep that night, I felt deeply unsettled by it all. For a less churchy experiment just try sitting sour faced on the front row of a comedy club or try reading a book at a large sports event.

When we see people around us acting in a certain way it is hard not to follow suit. Social conformity is a powerful way of producing compliance especially when it comes from those we trust. Other forces may be added too to ensure behavioral compliance. Public or private shaming, public or private commendation, both may be used to inflate or deflate us as required.

As we feel the mounting pressure we increasingly engage with the cultic system.

Activities within the system also serve to undermine our judgement. Meditation or prayer can be used to instil hypnotic states that make us more susceptible to the “truth” of the message. The same can be true of the TV, cinema or internet. I often get a similar feeling watching adverts in the cinema. Loud music plays as a man walks on a beam of light through space. Car adverts mix surreal impossible images.

They serve to create dreamlike hypnotic states. This is clearly a minor pressure when it comes to persuasion but consider how much is spent each year on advertising.

As we sink deeper and deeper into the cult our language starts to change. Words that once seemed ordinary take on a new meaning not understood by an outsider. Each cult develops its own Orwellian Newspeak as the language becomes loaded.

Eventually we become a part of the system and apply to others the same coercive techniques that were used on us.

The end product of this process is the creation of phobias. Deep irrational fears linked with key beliefs held by the group. They seem crazy to any outsider but to those in the cult they have the capacity to invoke pure terror.

In my story the date was 1994.

The Birmingham Church of Christ was a 45 minute drive down the motorway. My wife and I had left the mainstream church in Leicester. Initially we traveled to Birmingham at least twice a week.

I was already pretty damaged. I had worked so hard and achieved so little. Mentally and emotionally drained I really needed time and space to recover.

I got that, for a little while – sort of.

When we arrived at the first meeting an older couple latched onto us and we became good friends. We often stayed with them on a Saturday and ate Sunday lunch together after the meeting. In spite of the age gap in my case I felt better able to relate to them, the younger members I found quite irritating.

We wanted to understand how they did it. We wanted to learn. How was it that the church in Leicester and the church in London, started around the same time could display such different patterns of growth?

What we were yet to fully appreciate is that there was a system, a machine. A machine that claims it will fill you with spirituality and bring you closer to God. It does no such thing. Dismantle the machine and compare it to other cults and you find the same components serving similar functions.

One core idea at the heart of this group was the idea of being a “disciple”. Here lay an important question. A disciple is a learner, so how quickly should a person learn? Should a disciple learn at their own speed, or should they learn quickly as dictated by external forces?

The group took the latter option.

A disciple was defined as somebody who is “totally committed”, “fired up for God”. If you were not a true disciple you were going to Hell.

(Note the loaded language.)

So what happens if I get up late one morning and want to take a day off from my morning prayer? Does that mean I fall below the standard of total commitment? Does that therefore make me a bad person? What if I refuse to learn something or disagree with a teaching? Does that make me rebellious? Am I a sinner?

The answer to these questions was invariable “yes”.

From the concept of being a disciple comes the first point of practice.

Every member of the group had to have a mentor called a discipler. A discipler was allocated over you to provide friendship and support; at least that is the theory. The discipling system forms a pyramid. Each person has another member over them and this reaches all the way to the top of the organisation.

Upon first arriving we were allocated the preacher and his wife to "disciple" us.

Any instruction given to you by your discipler was referred to as “advice” (more loaded language). It soon started to become clear that saying no was never an acceptable response to “advice”. If you were genuinely committed and had faith you must always answer in the affirmative.

I was still looking for work at this point in time. My typical day involved looking at job sites and going through the papers. Then the tedium would kick in. To alleviate this I would walk down to the local video shop. During the day they had a deal where you could rent six videos for a couple of quid. Mostly I ended up ploughing through the complete works of Jean-Claude Van Damme along with other cinema classics. In many ways my day was boring and exhausting but it did give me a dose of quality time with myself, something I always appreciate to re-charge my batteries and work out which way was up.

The preacher did not approve. Cults don’t like you disconnecting yourself from the group. Staying close to the pack is the best strategy for maintaining control. I didn’t have the strength or the inclination to disagree. His “advice”? I should drive over to Birmingham each day. This would be an opportunity to meet with the “brothers”. This is not brother in the sense of a monastic order, not brother in the sense of a trade union, it felt more like brother in the sense of some rap video, often shortened to bro’.

Each day I would travel to Birmingham. There was a great deal of talk about commitment. Anything perceived as a half measure was frowned upon. You had to be prepared to do whatever it takes to prove your commitment to the Lord. There would be long conversations about how you had to submit to your discipler in order to demonstrate what a "good heart" you had. I often talked to people with deep feelings of guilt and shame from simply wanting to assert their own identities.

Part of this commitment requires being completely submissive.

Given my upbringing to turn the other cheek I was pretty good at this!

Your discipler has been placed over you in a position of authority by the church. This authority obviously comes from God. This authority must therefore be obeyed or suffer the consequences. It was often unspoken but there was always a sense that something very bad could happen to you should you resist “advice”.

In one meeting for married couples it was announced that husbands have total authority over their wives and she must obey him even when he is wrong. Failure to obey your husband is failure to obey God. This was very much an extension of the authoritarian nature of the wider discipling system.

One day I was chatting to a “brother” and he asked how my job hunting was going, “not well I said”. He came up with the idea that I should offer my services for free, become a sort of intern. It wasn’t that bad an idea we had enough money coming in so it was a viable option.

I wrote to a load of local companies and within a few weeks I was offered a job at a local software house. The did agree to pay me too.

This brought the daily trips to Birmingham to an end.

The heart of the machine is discipling.

The fires of Hell fuel the machine.

The church would argue that the single function of being a Christian is to reach out to the lost.

If Hell is real and we have the means to save people then how selfish are you to keep the message to yourself?

"I don’t care if you don’t like talking to people, 'repent' and preach the word."

"Do you feel tired? It doesn’t matter, what about those people you have to reach? They are going to burn."

"Missions need money too. Holding back your finances is selfish when there is a world to save."

Any form of resistance is “sin”. You know where sin gets you? "Repent."

The church had a mission. That mission was to spread the "gospel" to the entire planet in a single generation. By the gospel I mean the gospel as defined by the ICC. Keep in mind they believe that every other church is wrong and are all going directly to Hell.

We have now added into the mix an alarming sense of urgency.

The pressure was overwhelming.

We were now a permanent fixture in Leicester. So the "advice" was that we needed to be bringing visitors from Leicester over to Birmingham every Sunday. There was one other member living locally and most nights after work we would meet at the clock tower and invite people to the meetings.

I conformed because everybody else conformed.

It was quite surprising how many people we managed to interest.

Another matter the group was very interested in was how much money we donated in contribution. We were “encouraged” to give ten percent of our earnings; ten percent of gross that is. This wasn’t the end of the matter though; there were also special contributions that had to be made at other times too. This was on top of the ten percent you were already giving. "This was a sacrifice demonstrating your true commitment to the Lord". There were cases of members signing up as medical guinea pigs for cash. The money raised was donated directly to the church.

How would they know what a person did or didn’t do with their own money?

Like Orwell’s book there was surveillance.

Another function of a discipler is to police the person they are discipling.

Six months in we were no longer being discipled by the preachers but by members further down the pyramid. They were fine as people but above them were a couple who were serious true believers who took little truck with any perceived lack of commitment. Those discipling the two of us clearly felt bullied and they passed that stress down the chain of command.

Every day there would be a phone call with my discipler. Then the intrusive questions would start. You had to be “open” and “vulnerable” to show you had a “good heart”. Disciplers would keep track of how much you contributed, how often you attended, who you were bringing to meetings. Start to slip in any of these areas and the questions would become tense and awkward. You knew your commitment was coming into question. Did you reach out to somebody today? Did you have your “quiet time” and get up early to pray? Did you read your Bible? Single men would be asked if they masturbated due to impure thoughts. Married couples would be asked about their sex life. Resistance was futile. "Advice" would cover such things as what to do with your time in the week, when to take a holiday, who to date, who to marry and where to work.

One final question. “Are you fired up?”

It wasn't good enought to simply obey, you had to do it with a smile on your face. You had to be excited, you had to sing with all your heart, pray as loud as you can, clap and shout in all the right places. By this process joy and enthusiasm are artificially synthesised even though you might feel dead and empty inside.

When everybody got the message there was a loud “amen!”

They were always chasing the perfect guilt trip within you.

By reaching this point you could be coerced into conformity and obedience. If there were no obvious sins to latch onto they could be invented. Old sins would be dragged from the past to remind you just how crap you really are.

Unconditional love of any sort, was completely off the menu.

Values of any real importance became lost in a cacophony of fear and obedience.

I did say "no" on one occasion. It was a tiny victory though. We were on holiday in Cornwall and my wife’s brother and brother in law were being appointed as elders in the London Church. The “advice” was that we “should try to be there”, so we drove all the way from Cornwall to London to attend the meeting on a Sunday. At the end of the meeting one of the leaders told us that we should cut short our holiday by a week and come back home. I said no. The look on her face was like I had taken a huge shit on her carpet. Make a habit of refusal and you start to hit problems.

Firstly you might find yourself involved in a breaking session.

You might find yourself in a room with other members and they will rip into you in order to break your will. I never found myself on the receiving end of such a session but there was a moment where I came close. I did know people who experienced this and talk of such sessions was common.

Secondly you may just find yourself placed under so much pressure by your discipler that you are simply forced to leave.

Additionally there were the purges. From time to time there had been a purge of members who were not sufficiently committed. Breaking sessions and pressure from disciplers would be used to clear out the dead wood.

The end point was to force further conformity or your ejection from the group. The latter option would result in disconnection from everybody you know and care for.

These brutal tools had power beyond even their implementation. They were waved around as a potential punishment for disobedience, often spoken in whispers. Even these whispers helped to keep members in line. Such threats were used like severed heads on a castle wall, a warning to the disobedient.

People were reluctant to test the system too far. They knew the risks as they would feel like they were betraying God as well as the group!

By now the inevitable phobias were well developed in me. There was a deep irrational terror of life on the outside. Leaving meant automatic entry into the flames of Hell and the loss of all I held dear. I didn't feel like I had sins, I felt like I was sin. Sin to the very core of my being, an utterly revolting person.

You were strongly encouraged to confess sins to your discipler. Whatever you said was not private to you and your discipler. Words said in private and actions done in public were passed up the pyramid. The people in charge knew who was doing what.

All of this information travelling up the system formed statistics or rather “the stats”. “The stats” were used to assess and judge the spiritual health of an individual, a study group, an entire church, even the whole movement.

An Excel spreadsheet sat in the judgement seat of God.

It held information on the number of visitors, the number of baptisms and the amount of contribution.

“Bow down and worship the great and mighty stats”.

What about the Bible? Like other previous groups the Bible is used as a proof text to justify the churches already established policies and procedures. The members learn a tiny subset of passages really well but with no context or academic rigor. The Bible was used as a weapon to bludgeon existing members and potential converts into compliance.

At the core of their beliefs was a profound and merciless pragmatism. Practice must be true because it gets results, the end justifies the means.

Anybody who gets in the way is simply collateral damage.

Over a period of about six months the kid’s gloves were off and we were set to work. Compliance was the only acceptable option. Meeting the impossible target of saving the world was the only priority.

Forget your needs, forget your wants, forget your desires, they no longer matter. Pick up your cross and walk in the footsteps of Jesus.

"He who gains his life will lose it; he who loses his life will find it."

I had started from a low point when I arrived; the only way from here was down.

There was a slow and steady decent into bitter darkness.

I also knew this was bullshit.

The tipping point came on a Wednesday not long after my birthday. I had recently met a chap from Mexico. I was advised to arrange a Bible study with him as soon as possible.

“Why not do it tonight after the Wednesday meeting?”

It wasn't really a question, nor was the motive pure.

By rushing the study through it would make it onto this weeks stats report.

“I don’t doubt your commitment Matthew.”

I did a full day at work, drove to Birmingham and back to Leicester. It was gone 10pm by the time we met for the Bible study, midnight by the time I was home.

I was so tired.

The next day I started with a bad cold. I telephoned into work sick.

The cold refused to lift. I felt terrible so went to see the GP. Immediately I was signed off with severe depression.

It was the start of six years sitting in a dark pit.
