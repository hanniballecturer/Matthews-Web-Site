Part 2 Turning the Other Cheek

The world had ended, I was now doomed to live in post apocalyptic wastelands. It was also the start of an eternal childhood summer holiday, there was seemingly an endless pause before starting my new school.

Some of the gloom of earlier years was lifting, but there were changes in the wind. We now lived in a two story flat above a shop. Older siblings were growing up and leaving.

I suppose we lived in quite a good area in Crewe, we did after all buy the house for a thousand pounds. In school there I don’t recall having to fight! After infants school I moved to a newly built school. It was modern and I remember being very happy there. The children were friendly and the teachers were positive and nurturing. The most terrifying event I recall was going out of the wrong door at lunch time and having to walk for what seemed like ages to get to the playground. I look back on those times with some warmth.

All that was gone now, there was a new terrain to navigate. The family had relocated, in part to rehabilitate my dad after his illness. We were also escaping the Christadelphian meeting house. Dad's illness had resulted in him no longer attending. Rather than responding with the compassion one might expect from a supposedly Christian group, pressure was applied to either attend or be thrown out.

I remember that bus ride from Crewe to Congleton wondering what was going on, I really didn’t understand, Congleton seemed like the other side of the earth.

I do remember it feeling awkward to fit in with the children around the estate. In part it was me, I had no idea how to behave with these kids, I know at times I was deliberately obtuse. A bigger part of it was them though, they were so violent and aggressive.

Open arriving at the new school, I was horrified by the difference in culture. Fighting was verging on some sort of currency, with a pecking order in place.

There was a term used, “the cock”. The “cock” was the biggest hardest thug in a given domain. There was a “cock” for each year and there was an alleged “cock of the school”. The “cock” was simply the boy who could defeat everybody else in a fight. The fighting seemed almost systematised. There were constant challenges to the title, constant posturing and jockeying for pole position. The teachers were not much better. They seemed to have a certain talent for meanness of spirit and a sharpness of tongue.

I remember very few specific incidents of violence. They happened, but the prime focus was on ritual humiliation. I was sent to school in shorts FFS! Just add a flat cap to complete the look of an escapee from the work house!

In retrospect I am not sure which was more damaging; the environment into which I was plunged, or the advice I was given. I must have told my parents about how unhappy I was otherwise they would not have offered their wisdom. “It was wrong to fight back”, “two wrongs don’t make a right” and always remember the words of Jesus, to “turn the other cheek”. I find this is common advice from Christadelphians in response to conflict, violence and abuse.

The fight or flight response is both normal and healthy and part of what keeps us safe from danger. This advice didn’t do anything to address the cruelty of my classmates, the danger still remained. It undermined my fight response leaving me with only one option, that of flight. I stopped going to school. Most of my junior school education and a year of my senior school were missed; I was too scared to go!

In truth though, I quite liked being at home all of the time.

The school did give me work to do, which at times I did, but mostly I did my own thing, building experiments, writing, drawing, watching educational programs on the TV. I hated playing out. The children at the school could no longer get at me in the school yard, when they met me in the street they seemed even more nasty.

My first encounter with "counselling" took place during that era too. I was sent to a child psychologist to try and work out what was wrong with me. It now seems blindingly obvious what was wrong, I was a child trying to make sense of this mess, and the problem was never me!

In time the school system lost its patience. I was told in no uncertain terms that I had to start going to school or I would be put into care. This scared the crap out of me and was a massive incentive to attend school.

It was a bumpy start, I was so far behind in my work. I was in senior school by now. This was another rough school with fights every break time. The same bunch of thugs from junior school had followed me here. It wasn’t quite the same though. I was streamed with boys from other schools who were easier to relate to. Mostly the thugs were kept well away from me; sometimes you would see them dragging their knuckles on the floor as they slope off to classes on drain digging or some such.

Yes the school started as a rough school with a bad reputation, but with the installation of a new Head, the strategic retirement of “certain” teachers and the employment of teachers who wanted to actually teach, the school became a really positive place.

Two things happened, the threat level decreased and at some point I went against the eleventh commandment “thou shalt not fight back”.

It came as quite a shock to some of the thugs when I kicked the crap out of them. I didn’t need to do it that too often to gain a reputation. After all, what better way to make a “cock” look like a "total cock" than them being beaten up by me? I found that fighting back was empowering and something I could do if required. This new assertive me was able to perform at school sports, get top grades and gain the support of my peers.

I still remember one day walking down the street wearing my Dr Martin boots and sage green Parka jacket feeling really good about myself.

Then one day somebody told me I was "cock" of the year.

Suddenly I felt I had overcompensated, becoming verbally and physically aggressive. I was full of hormones, I hit puberty ahead of my peers, and coupled with my added height it gave me an added edge. Then the religious guilt kicked in. “If this is what it means to be assertive then perhaps it is better to turn the other cheek”. I had no proper concept of assertiveness, I didn’t realise that there was a third way between aggression and compliance.

And then my dad died.

I was 13.

And then there was anger and shame.

I felt I was acting independently of my family and God. I felt I was becoming exactly the type of person I hated. Grieving, guilty and isolated, passivity seemed better than aggression, the threat level started to rise again and once more I was easy prey.

And then came depression.

I felt angry, defenceless and vulnerable. I was also covered in spots and was now failing in a new competitive arena related to girls. I lost interest in my A levels and withdrew into computers and guitars as my flight response came to the fore again.

The end of the world had a massive impact, but in some ways "turn the other cheek" had a much greater one.
