Part 1 Apocalypse 1975

It was the year of my birth when the prophecy was declared, the end of the world was coming in 1975.
When that year arrived I think for me in some sense the end did come.

To say that life growing up was gloomy would be something of an understatement.  I was raised in a pre-millennial religious cult called the Christadelphians (CDs for the sake of typing sanity).  As a group they spend a lot of time waiting (lots of waiting) for the return of Jesus and the coming kingdom of God. This return will require the incineration of the majority of the global population. (This of course depends on if you are a CD or not, after all, they are the only ones who really have "the truth").

I am still not 100% clear to what extent the CDs are “officially” dispensationalists it never gets mentioned in the advertising. Dispensationalists believe that the history of mankind from the creation and Adam is split up into time periods following the structure of the seven days of creation.  Typically if a group believes in a young earth then the return of Jesus and the end of all things has to be some time about now(ish) – probably - hopefully (as I said lots of waiting).  I am still not clear to what extent the CDs officially follow this view but I certainly remember mum sharing her thoughts on the subject with me.  Unlike the Jehovah’s Witnesses (JWs – again for typing sanity), the CDs have (for the most part) resisted placing an actual date on Armageddon. The JWs have made a few efforts to predict the end of the world all obviously wrong, at which point the leadership whistles in the air and moves swiftly on as if it never happened. I know the JWs are now prone to deny the whole 1975 prophecy debacle and I have zero interest in debating this, the evidence is so far as I can see is conclusive that this was case, go look it up.

Really though, I don’t give a shit about dispensationalist beliefs, I couldn’t care less about the truth or otherwise of prophecy.  What I do care about is the impact crappy beliefs have on a person's mental and emotional health and ultimately the quality of their lives.

I remember one day looking out of the front window in the house in Crewe.  I could see people walking by who I knew were not believers. I must have been around seven finding it frightening to think that God was going to wipe them all out. There was no pleasure in this.

On one hand it seemed normal as I didn't know anything else, as I have grown older I more clearly see the damage done. It is so hard to express to outsiders how utterly toxic the environment was.  It was the cold war; Russians were a constant threat on the horizon the fear of nuclear holocaust was always alive.  If God was to burn up the Earth and start anew perhaps humans would be the ones who would do his dirty work for him by dropping the bomb.   Even as a child I was profoundly aware of the evils of the current age and its imminent destruction.  There was little hope for the future; even God’s Kingdom post apocalypse didn’t seem too grand.  The idea of an eternity with the CDs didn’t look much like fun from where I was standing.

Add to this gloom my father.  He was a dark figure by then.  I do remember times when he was fun and would tickle me till it was agony. Sadly I also remember being told to leave him alone as he sat looking shadowy and exhausted in his chair with a mug of tea.

We all lived in the darkness of the end times.  Hal Lindsey’s The Late Great Planet Earth was, can you believe, the number one selling book of the 1970’s?  Hal Lindsey didn’t opt for 1975 as the year for the end but reading that book on publication in 1970 would have made the coming decade a very gloomy prospect.

Now factor in the 1975 prediction.  As an adult I am in the process of piecing together the order of events of my early childhood.  Through the eyes of that child it all seems disconnected and muddled.  By drawing up a time-line I can get a better sense of what events happened when, and most of all how old we all were. As an adult I understand now more clearly the impact of bereavement, separation and loss, lessons my young self would soon learn. I was second to the youngest and my older siblings all seemed like grownups.  This though was the illusion of childhood, they were not grownups, we were all kids and young teenagers trying to find our way in this pretty toxic environment.

My dad’s brother and his wife were JWs.  They were not fondly regarded by our family as they took certain actions that were financially beneficial to themselves resulting in my parents having to look after my dad’s mother. I know my parents studied with my uncle and aunty deciding instead to join the CDs over the JWs. I find it hard to believe, given the similarities of the two cults, that conversations were not ongoing over the coming years about their respective belief systems.

So, what about 1975?  In 1966 the announcement was made by the Watchtower that the world was going to end in 1975.  Now is the period to prepare for the battle of Armageddon.  Given the organisational structure of the JWs this announcement didn’t take long to make it over the Atlantic.  A quick search on YouTube will bring up a 1968 BBC radio broadcast with a member of the time explaining the end of the world in 75.

Members sold valuable possessions, they sold houses, they gave up jobs, they delayed education, couples decided not to start families and not undertake healthcare all in an effort to invest time and money into the mission of converting as many as possible during the lead up to the end.  This prediction perhaps starts to shed a new light on the actions of my dad’s brother and his wife.

Then there was my dad’s illness.  There was a long lead up of other contributing factors, but the worst period started around 1968, ending - oddly enough after we moved to Congleton around guess when? 1975.  Perhaps it was all just a coincidence, dad wasn't better overnight it took a long time, in truth he was never fully well.  Perhaps being a pre-millennial dispensationalist with a JW brother between 1966 and 1975 had no impact at all and the subject was never raised?

But what about me?  I had a nice school in Crewe, friends, familiar haunts and a familiar home.  It seemed I came home one day and was suddenly transported by bus into hell. I was now in a rough estate and a rough school feeling like I had landed from Mars.  I had no idea how to relate to the violent bully culture in the school and my parent’s sage advice to always “turn the other cheek” only served to make matters worse. Looking back now I feel in many ways that for me the world really had ended in 1975.
