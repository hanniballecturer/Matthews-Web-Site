Introduction (Why are my students failing?

Originally starting work in HE as a teacher of computing related topics, I quickly noticed high failure rates in subjects, notably programming.

It used to bug the hell out of me.

In part this was down to the fact that running a university module with failure rates above a certain threshold brought you to the attention of the “powers that be” in not necessarily a good way. I also found it personally irritating.

My natural instincts however are to try and untangle great big Gordian Knots and this seemed like a biggie.

Why is it that no matter what I seem to do, topics such as programming seem to come with a very long tail of bad grades, perhaps akin to a snake.

My initial theory was as follows…

“The students are thick”.

It made sense, and it did make me feel better and it did help at least to shift to the blame, it also helped to build close ties to other like-minded colleagues.

But there was still a nagging feeling that there may be some part of the story missing in this explanation.

I often found myself reflecting on my own story.

In the early 1980’s having failed most of my A Levels I set out to a place called Leicester (I believed was in the northeast of England) to embark on a two-year HND in Applied Biology. By the end of the first year, I was on the verge of being thrown off the course, but this was resolved by a friend on the teaching team advising the assessment board of “mitigating circumstances”.

Whilst there was a lot going on for me at that point in my life to be fair looking back, I am not sure going to the pub every night for much of my first year was helping nor could it really be described as “mitigation”. However, it gave me a second chance, and I continued to the second year of the course. I did OK with the added stability I was finding, but by the end I was done with Biology. I now had a clear idea that it wasn’t for me. After this I messed around for quite a while in rubbish sales jobs and the dole.

Here is the thing.

Somewhere around the age of 15 I had to select my options for school. I wanted to do Art and possibly Music, I really wanted to do something involving Computers given I was the only person I knew who owned one (A Sinclair ZX80 eventually ZX81 and then Spectrum)

But no!

I could predict the response from my mum on the Arts/Music front but was also explicitly told “you won’t be able to make a career in computers; there will never be any money in it.”

So, I opted for Biology, Physics and Chemistry. I was supposed to carry on to a happy career in Chemistry in the glamorous locales of Runcorn and Widnes working at ICI. As my first year in the lower 6th progressed by Christmas I had given up Physics as I just couldn’t keep up with the complexity, and by the end of the 6th form I had failed Chemistry and squeaked a “pass” in Biology, so I was handed my “go directly to HND Biology card”, do not pass Runcorn, do not collect £100 from each player.

Was I stupid?

If you asked me back then I may have agreed.

But looking back I know that I wasn’t. During 30ish years in academia I have done well thank you.

I went down the pure science route because I thought that was what I was supposed to do. I delayed going down the computing route because I was discouraged on financial grounds.

And it was this personal experience that was really bugging me with me students.

What if like me they were not stupid but for what ever reasons they were pursuing paths for completely incorrect and unappealing motives?

So, off the back of this I decided to investigate how people learn and what motivates them to learn.

I took a PgCert HE, I became a Teaching Fellow, I completed an MA in Education Research always at the back of my mind trying to get a grip on this question “why do students fail?”

Any you know what after all those years all that study and all those qualifications and all that hard work, you know what?

I honestly believe I still missed it.

Three years ago, I was fortunate enough to take early retirement, but the fact is there is still this burning ember in my brain surrounding this question of student failure.

I still read around the topic and then something dawned on me.

What if the problem isn’t intelligence, what if the problem is imagination?

I have spent quite a while sitting with this idea, the more I think about it the more I find that it opens a whole new way of framing the world.

There are a couple of instant observations to note.

If the imagination is central to human life and endeavour, then why do we not spend more time talking about it? It seems to me that for most part around the age of 15 we are essentially told “stop dreaming and get with the programme”. This suggest that the imagination is at some level incompatible with being an adult.

In addition, if as an adult you do pursue a career in obviously imaginative pursuits like the arts you will always have one eye in the mirror as you see a “proper job” tailing you should this whole “creative thing” lead to poverty.

So, this sends a clear message that the imagination is solely the domain of children, artists who can afford it and loonies.

But if academic failure for example is linked with the imagination and not intelligence then perhaps, we need to really talk about the imagination and the role it plays a lot more.

The other item of note is to look with wonder at the role that imagination has played in the rise of humans as a species.

Animals make tools, from birds’ nests, to apes using stones to crush seeds.

Humans however are the only animal on the planet to look at two stones and use one to sharpen the other and create a flint knife. They then use that knife to cut dried animal skin and wood, add a suitable stone and you have a basic sling shot.

We don’t just make tools; we imagine new ways of making new tools out of the tools we already have. This creates a direct line from the first flint axe to modern AI and genetic engineering.

So…. I feel a need to organise my thoughts on this. Initially in an informal manner as a blog but perhaps more formally as something else as I refine my ideas.

Some of the writings will be tentative perhaps even wrong but this is to some degree a data dump of ideas that are now no longer a burning ember but a source of some excitement and even perhaps discomfort at the rising mental temperature.
